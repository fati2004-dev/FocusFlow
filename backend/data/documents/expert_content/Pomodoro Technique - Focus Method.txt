Pomodoro Technique - Focus Method

## Overview
The Pomodoro Technique is a time management method developed by Francesco Cirillo in the late 1980s. It uses a timer to break work into intervals, traditionally 25 minutes in length, separated by short breaks. "Pomodoro" means tomato in Italian, named after the tomato-shaped kitchen timer Cirillo used.

## The 5 Steps
1. **Choose a task** to work on
2. **Set timer for 25 minutes** (one Pomodoro)
3. **Work on the task** until the timer rings (no interruptions!)
4. **Take a short break** (5 minutes)
5. **After 4 Pomodoros**, take a longer break (15-30 minutes)

## Why It Works
- **Creates urgency** - Limited time creates focus
- **Reduces distractions** - Committed to focus for short period
- **Manages mental energy** - Regular breaks prevent burnout
- **Provides structure** - Clear work-rest rhythm
- **Builds momentum** - Easy to start knowing it's only 25 minutes

## Rules for Success
- **One Pomodoro = one task** - No multitasking
- **If interrupted**: 
  - Internal interruption (thought/urge): Write it down, continue Pomodoro
  - External interruption: Ask to wait, or abandon Pomodoro if urgent
- **Protect the Pomodoro** - It's sacred focus time
- **Track your Pomodoros** - Count how many per day for self-awareness

## Variations
- **Customizable intervals**: 25/5 (traditional), 50/10 (deep work), 15/3 (easy entry)
- **Flowtime Technique**: Work until natural break, then rest
- **Group Pomodoros**: Teams working together with shared timer

## Benefits
- Increased focus and concentration
- Reduced anxiety about large tasks
- Improved time estimation skills
- Clear record of work accomplished
- Prevention of burnout through structured breaks
